# The management of patients with polycystic ovary syndrome

PCOS is a very common endocrine disorder, with a spectrum of clinical presentation. In addition to the traditional
aims of treating hirsutism and disordered menstrual function, numerous studies have established that patients with
PCOS are at increased risk of T2DM and have risk factors for the development of cardiovascular disease. Hirsutism typically requires management by a combination
of cosmetic (hair removal) methods and suppression of
both androgen secretion and action (by administration
of combined oral contraceptives with or without supplementary anti-androgen therapy). Infertility in PCOS is
a function of oligo-ovulation or anovulation. In patients
who are overweight or have obesity, lifestyle interventions,
principally diet and exercise, might result in resumption
of ovulation. However, most infertile women with PCOS
will require induction of ovulation by, in the first instance,
anti-estrogen therapy, followed, if necessary, by low-dose
gonadotropin treatment or laparoscopic ovarian diathermy.
Menstrual irregularity in women who do not wish to conceive can best be managed by treatment with a combined
oral contraceptive or cyclical progestogens. Metformin
might improve the frequency of ovulation but is not a reliable agent for treatment of infertility either as monotherapy
or in combination with clomiphene.
Lifestyle interventions such as weight loss and exercise
remain the most effective means of lowering cardiovascular risk and reducing progression to diabetes mellitus, as
well as regulating periods. If lifestyle interventions prove
unsuccessful, metformin is an effective second-line treatment to reduce conversion of IGT to T2DM, even though
its benefits as an agent for the treatment of infertility in
women with PCOS are not proven. The most important
consideration of management is to tailor treatment choices
to the specific needs of the patient.
